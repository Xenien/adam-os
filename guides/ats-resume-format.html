ATS Resume Format: The 8 Rules That Get You Past the Software

Before anyone judges your experience, software judges your formatting. Over 90% of large employers screen applications with an applicant tracking system, and a resume the parser can't read cleanly gets ranked as if the missing parts don't exist. These are the eight rules that matter — each one is a check JobFit runs on every scan.

Rule 1 — Contact info the parser can find

Put your email address and phone number in plain text near the top of the document. Not in a header or footer (many parsers skip those), not in a graphic, not spelled creatively. If the system can't extract a way to reach you, nothing else on the resume matters. Add your city and a LinkedIn URL on the same lines; recruiters expect both.

Rule 2 — Standard section headings

Parsers identify your sections by their names. "Summary," "Experience" (or "Work Experience"), "Education," "Skills," plus "Projects" or "Certifications" if you need them — these are recognized everywhere. Inventive headings like "My Journey" or "What I Bring" can cause a parser to misfile or drop entire sections. Aim for at least three or four conventional headings; it's the skeleton the software hangs everything else on.

Rule 3 — A date range on every role

The ATS builds an employment timeline from the years it finds. Every position needs a clear range — "2021 – 2024" or "Mar 2021 – Jun 2024" — on the same line or the line below the title. A role without dates may be dropped from your work history entirely, and a resume with almost no detectable years reads to the software like a resume with no experience.

Rule 4 — The right length: 400–800 words

Length is a real ranking factor, in both directions. Under about 350 words, there simply isn't enough text to match the posting's keywords, and you rank low on surface area alone. Over about 900 words — usually past two pages — your relevant experience gets diluted by everything else. The sweet spot is 400–800 words, achieved by cutting your oldest and least relevant material, not by shrinking the font.

Rule 5 — Quantified achievements

Numbers are the strongest signal on a resume — for the human reader above all, but increasingly for ranking algorithms too. Percentages, dollar amounts, and counts ("increased retention 18%," "managed a $2M budget," "served 50+ clients a week") turn claims into evidence. Aim for at least three quantified results, and more if your field produces them naturally. If a bullet has no number, ask: how many, how much, how often, compared to what?

Rule 6 — Bullets that start with action verbs

"Led," "built," "reduced," "negotiated," "shipped." Starting each bullet with a strong verb does two jobs: it forces you to write about outcomes instead of duties, and it reads instantly in the six seconds a recruiter gives a first pass. Aim for five or more bullets that open with an action verb. The pattern to break is the passive job-description voice: "Was responsible for managing..." becomes "Managed..."

Rule 7 — No weak phrases

Certain phrases mark a resume as generic the moment they're read: "responsible for," "duties included," "team player," "hard worker," "go-getter," "think outside the box," "results-driven professional," "references available upon request." None of them are keywords any posting screens for, and every one of them spends space that a real skill or real number could occupy. Delete them and replace each with the specific accomplishment it was gesturing at.

Rule 8 — Professional resume voice: no "I," "me," or "my"

Resumes are written in implied first person: "Led a team of 5," not "I led a team of 5." A stray pronoun or two won't sink you, but a resume written in full sentences about yourself reads like a cover letter and wastes words the keyword match could be using. This is the easiest rule to fix — delete the pronouns and let the verbs carry the sentence.

What these rules don't cover

Formatting gets you parsed correctly — it doesn't get you ranked highly. Ranking is dominated by how well your wording matches the specific posting's wording: the skills, tools, and methods it names. That's a separate problem with a separate method, covered in How to find the right resume keywords for any job.
