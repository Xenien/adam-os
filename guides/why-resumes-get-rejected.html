Why Your Resume Gets Rejected by ATS — and How to Fix It

You applied to forty jobs, you're qualified for most of them, and you heard back from two. That silence usually isn't a hiring manager passing on you — it's screening software that never showed you to one.

The filter you never see

An applicant tracking system (ATS) is the software a company uses to collect, parse, and rank applications. Over 90% of large employers use one, and roughly 75% of resumes are filtered out before a human reads them. The system isn't reading your resume the way a person would. It extracts text, tries to identify sections (experience, education, skills), and then ranks you — mostly on how well your wording matches the wording of the job posting.

That last part is the piece most job seekers miss. The ATS is not evaluating whether you can do the job. It's measuring whether the words on your resume overlap with the words in the posting. A brilliant candidate who describes their experience in different vocabulary than the posting loses to an average candidate who mirrors it.

The five reasons resumes get cut

1. Keyword mismatch — the big one

Postings name skills, tools, and methods precisely: "stakeholder management," "Salesforce," "A/B testing," "electronic health records." Screening software searches for those exact phrases. If the posting says "email marketing" and your resume says "ran email campaigns," you may not match. If it says "EHR" and you wrote "charted patient records," you don't match at all — even though you plainly have the skill.

The fix is translation, not fabrication: for every key term in the posting that is genuinely true of you, use the posting's exact wording somewhere in your resume — a skills entry, an experience bullet, your summary. Never claim a skill you don't have; the keyword gets you to the interview, where a human will ask about it.

2. Formatting the parser can't read

Before ranking you, the ATS has to parse your resume into structured data. Tables, multi-column layouts, text boxes, headers and footers, and graphics can scramble that step — your dates end up attached to the wrong job, or a whole section silently disappears. A clean single-column document with conventional headings (Summary, Experience, Education, Skills) parses reliably everywhere. Our companion guide, ATS resume format: the 8 rules, covers each formatting check in detail.

3. Missing or unparseable basics

Some rejections are purely mechanical. No detectable email or phone number near the top. Job entries without date ranges, so the system can't build your work timeline. Section headings so creative ("Where I've Made an Impact") that the parser doesn't recognize them as experience. Each of these is a two-minute fix that removes a silent disqualifier.

4. A resume that's too thin — or too bloated

Very short resumes lose on raw surface area: fewer words means fewer chances to match the posting's terms. A resume under about 350 words rarely ranks well. The opposite failure is stuffing three pages with everything you've ever done, which buries your relevant experience in noise. The reliable range is roughly 400–800 words, tailored to the one posting in front of you.

5. Weak language that survives the ATS but dies with the recruiter

Passing the software isn't the finish line — a recruiter spends seconds on each resume that gets through. Bullets that open with "Responsible for..." or lean on clichés like "team player" and "hard worker" waste those seconds. Bullets that open with an action verb and end with a number ("Reduced onboarding time 30% by...") win them. The strongest resumes are written for both readers at once: exact keywords for the software, verbs and numbers for the human.

How to fix it — in order

  1. Pick one real posting. Tailoring beats broadcasting. A resume aimed at one job outranks a generic resume sent to fifty.
  2. Find the posting's key terms and mirror the true ones. Skills, tools, certifications, and methods it names — worked into your bullets in the posting's exact wording. Our guide to finding the right resume keywords walks through this step by step.
  3. Fix the mechanical failures. Contact info at the top, standard headings, a date range on every role, single-column layout, 400–800 words.
  4. Rewrite for the human. Action verbs first, numbers wherever they exist, clichés deleted.
  5. Check before you send. Don't guess whether you match — measure it.
