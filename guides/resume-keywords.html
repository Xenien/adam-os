How to Find the Right Resume Keywords for Any Job

"Add keywords to your resume" is the most repeated and least explained advice in job searching. Which words? From where? How many? This guide is the concrete version: what screening software actually treats as a keyword, how to extract the ones that matter from any posting, and where to put them.

What a keyword actually is

To an applicant tracking system, a keyword is a term from the job posting that it searches for in your resume — usually as an exact word or phrase. In practice they fall into four groups:

  • Hard skills and methods: "financial modeling," "A/B testing," "care coordination," "stakeholder management." These carry the most weight — they're the substance of the role.
  • Tools and systems: Salesforce, Excel, Epic, SolidWorks, Jira, Workday. Software names are the easiest terms for a machine to match, which is exactly why recruiters search on them.
  • Certifications and credentials: PMP, CPA, BLS, CDL, OSHA. Often used as hard filters — miss the term, miss the job.
  • Repeated domain vocabulary: whatever the posting keeps saying. A posting that mentions "onboarding" five times is telling you what the job is about, and the screen is likely tuned to it.

Notice what's not on the list: buzzwords. "Motivated," "dynamic," and "team player" are not keywords — no recruiter searches for them, and they spend space real terms could use.

The extraction method (10 minutes per posting)

  1. Work from the posting itself, not the job title. Two postings for the same title screen on different terms. The posting in front of you is the only source that matters.
  2. Highlight every named skill, tool, method, and credential. Go line by line through the responsibilities and requirements sections. If it's a proper noun or a term of art, it's a candidate keyword.
  3. Mark anything that appears more than once. Repetition is emphasis. A term the posting uses three times matters more than one it mentions in passing — weight your effort accordingly.
  4. Note two-word phrases, not just single words. Screening software matches "project management" as a phrase. Having "project" in one bullet and "management" in another is not a match.
  5. Capture both forms of every acronym. The posting may say "EHR" while the recruiter searches "electronic health records," or vice versa. Your resume should contain each certification and system spelled out once with the abbreviation beside it: "electronic health records (EHR)."

The honesty rule

Only use a keyword that is genuinely true of you. This isn't just ethics — it's strategy. Keywords get you past the software and into an interview, where a human will ask about every one of them. A resume that matches on skills you don't have converts rejections into wasted interviews. The right move with a missing keyword you truly lack is to leave it out — or go learn it.

Most of the time, though, the missing keyword is something you do have, described in different words. You did "full cycle recruiting" but wrote "hired staff." You did "inventory management" but wrote "counted and ordered stock." Translation — restating your real experience in the posting's vocabulary — is where almost all keyword gains come from.

Where keywords go

  • Skills section: the straightforward home for tools, systems, and hard skills. Keep it a plain list the parser can read.
  • Experience bullets: the strongest placement, because it shows the keyword in use with a result attached: "Automated monthly reporting in Excel, saving 6 hours a week."
  • Summary: your top three or four keywords, matched to the posting's own emphasis, in the first two lines a recruiter reads.
  • Certifications section: every credential, spelled out and abbreviated.

Don't paste keywords in white text, cram them into a wall at the bottom, or repeat one term a dozen times. Parsers flatten formatting tricks into visible text, and a keyword-stuffed resume that passes the software gets discarded by the first human who opens it. One natural use of a phrase is a match; the goal is coverage across the posting's terms, not repetition of one.

How many keywords do you need?

You will rarely match everything, and you don't need to. What matters is covering the terms the posting emphasizes — the heavily weighted skills, tools, and credentials — rather than hitting some raw count. In practice, moving from "wrote my resume once, sent it everywhere" to "translated my real experience into this posting's top ten terms" is the difference between silence and interviews.
