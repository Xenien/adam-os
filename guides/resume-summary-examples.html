Resume Summary Examples: Templates That Pass the Screen

The summary is the first thing a recruiter reads and the first text a parser extracts — which makes it the most valuable and most wasted real estate on most resumes. This guide gives you a three-line formula, fill-in-the-blank templates for six common situations, and the mistakes that turn a summary into filler. Every template here is original and generic: fill it with your own facts, never invented ones.

What a summary is for

A resume summary is two to four lines at the top of the document that state who you are professionally, what you're strongest at, and one piece of evidence. It does two jobs at once. For the human, it frames everything below it in the six seconds a first skim gets. For the screening software, it's prime keyword placement: your top three or four terms from the posting, in the first lines of extracted text. "Summary" is also one of the standard section headings parsers recognize — use that exact word, not "About Me" or "Profile Statement."

What a summary is not: an objective ("Seeking a challenging position where I can grow...") — that tells the employer what you want instead of what they get, and contains no keywords any system screens for.

The three-line formula

  1. Line 1 — identity: your job title (matched to the posting's title where honest) plus years of experience and your specialty.
  2. Line 2 — skills: your two or three strongest skills that the posting also asks for, in the posting's exact wording.
  3. Line 3 — proof: one quantified achievement, your best number.

The base template, with brackets for everything that must come from your real history:

"[Job title] with [N] years of experience in [specialty]. Strong background in [skill 1], [skill 2], and [skill 3]. [Achieved specific result with a number] at [type of company/setting]."

Templates for six situations

These are deliberately generic patterns — swap in your own titles, skills, and numbers. If a bracket doesn't apply to you, that template isn't your template.

Experienced professional, same field

"[Title] with [N]+ years of experience in [industry/specialty]. Deep expertise in [skill 1], [skill 2], and [tool the posting names]. [Delivered/led/grew] [specific quantified result], most recently as [current role level]."

Career changer

"[New target title] combining [N] years in [previous field] with [training/certification in new field]. Transferable strengths in [shared skill 1] and [shared skill 2], plus hands-on experience with [new-field tool or project]. [One quantified result from the old field that the new field values]."

The career changer's summary works when the skills named are the genuinely transferable ones — the terms both fields use. Lead with those, not with an apology.

Recent graduate

"[Degree] graduate with hands-on experience in [skill/tool] through [internships/projects/ part-time work]. Built [specific project or outcome with a number where possible]. Seeking to apply [skill 1] and [skill 2] as a [target title]."

A graduate's numbers come from projects, internships, and part-time work — team size, users, data volume, results measured in class or on the job. Small honest numbers beat large vague claims.

Returning to the workforce

"[Title] with [N] years of prior experience in [field], returning after [a career break]. Maintained [skill/credential] through [course, certification, freelance, or volunteer work]. Previously [quantified achievement from your last role]."

Stepping up to a bigger role

"[Current title] with [N] years of increasing responsibility in [specialty], including [leadership-adjacent evidence: mentoring, project lead, deputizing]. Strong record in [skill 1] and [skill 2] — [best quantified result]. Ready to own [scope of the target role]."

Hands-on / skilled trades

"[Trade title], [license/certification], with [N] years across [settings: residential, commercial, industrial]. Skilled in [competency 1], [competency 2], and [competency 3 in the posting's words]. [Safety or quality record stated as a number]."

The mistakes that make summaries hurt

  • Buzzword strings. "Dynamic, results-driven professional and proven team player" contains zero keywords any posting screens for — phrases like "team player" and "results-driven professional" are on the weak-phrase list JobFit flags. Every word in the summary should be a word the posting uses or a number.
  • One summary for every application. Line 2 exists to mirror this posting's top terms. Two postings for the same title emphasize different skills; the summary is the cheapest place to retune. See how to find the right keywords for the extraction method.
  • First-person pronouns. "I am a marketing manager with..." — resumes use implied first person. Start at the title: "Marketing manager with..."
  • Borrowed facts. Templates are shapes, not biographies. A summary claiming years, tools, or results you don't have gets you interviews you can't survive. Fill every bracket from your real history.
  • Length. Four lines is the ceiling. Past that it's not a summary — and at 400–800 total resume words (see one page vs. two), you can't spare the space.

After the summary: the bullets have to deliver

A summary makes a promise the experience section has to keep. The same formula — skill in the posting's words plus a number — runs through every strong bullet, and the opening verb does the framing; see Resume action verbs for the full list and rewrite patterns.
