Resume Action Verbs: The Words That Make Bullets Work

The first word of every bullet decides how the whole line reads. Start with "Was responsible for..." and you've written a job description; start with "Reduced..." and you've written an accomplishment. This guide covers why the opening verb matters to both the software and the human, 80+ strong verbs grouped by what you're trying to say, and a formula for rewriting any weak bullet.

Why the first word of a bullet matters

Recruiters give a resume a famously short first pass, and in a bulleted document the words they actually see are the ones at the start of each line. A column of bullets opening with "Led... Built... Reduced... Negotiated..." reads as a track record before a single full sentence has been processed. A column opening with "Responsible for... Assisted with... Duties included..." reads as a copy-pasted job description.

Screening software cares too, in two ways. First, resume parsers and ranking algorithms treat achievement-shaped language as a quality signal — it's one of the eight checks JobFit runs on every scan, which looks for at least five bullets that open with a strong action verb. Second, and more practically: a bullet forced to start with a verb almost always ends up containing a real skill and a real result, which is where keyword matches and numbers — the two things that actually drive your ranking — come from. The verb isn't magic; it's the lever that pulls the rest of the sentence into shape.

The formula: verb + what + result

Every strong bullet has the same skeleton: an action verb, the specific thing you acted on (ideally in the posting's vocabulary), and a measurable result. Compare these generic templates:

  • Weak: "Was responsible for managing the company's social media accounts."
    Strong: "Grew [platform] following from [X] to [Y] in [N] months by [method]."
  • Weak: "Duties included handling customer complaints."
    Strong: "Resolved [N]+ customer escalations a week, maintaining a [X]% satisfaction score."
  • Weak: "Assisted with the monthly financial reporting process."
    Strong: "Automated the monthly reporting package in [tool], cutting close time from [X] to [Y] days."

Fill the brackets with your own numbers and tools — never anyone else's. The template is the shape; the truth is yours.

Action verbs by what you're trying to say

All of these are verbs JobFit's scan recognizes as strong openers. Pick the one that matches what actually happened — precision beats drama.

  • You led people or ran things: led · managed · directed · supervised · oversaw · coordinated · spearheaded · mentored · coached · trained · hired · recruited · facilitated
  • You made something new: built · created · developed · designed · launched · shipped · engineered · architected · authored · wrote · published · produced · established · founded · initiated
  • You made something better: improved · increased · grew · expanded · scaled · accelerated · boosted · optimized · streamlined · automated · modernized · transformed · redesigned · revamped · standardized · consolidated
  • You cut cost, time, or waste: reduced · decreased · cut · saved · eliminated
  • You won business or hit targets: generated · closed · negotiated · secured · won · earned · achieved · exceeded · delivered · drove · pitched · partnered
  • You figured something out: analyzed · researched · identified · diagnosed · resolved · solved · evaluated · audited · forecasted
  • You executed and kept things running: implemented · executed · deployed · migrated · integrated · maintained · tested · administered · organized · planned · monitored · tracked · budgeted · presented · advised · consulted · collaborated

The phrases to delete on sight

The mirror image of the action-verb check is the weak-phrase check. These constructions mark a resume as generic, match no keyword any posting screens for, and spend space a real skill or number could occupy: "responsible for," "duties included," "team player," "hard worker," "go-getter," "think outside the box," "results-driven professional," "references available upon request." Each one is hiding a real accomplishment — find it and write that instead. "Responsible for a team of five" was always "Led a team of 5."

Three mistakes to avoid

  1. Verb inflation. "Spearheaded" a meeting you attended, "transformed" a spreadsheet you updated — a verb your interview can't back up converts a rejection into a wasted interview. Pick the strongest verb that is still simply true.
  2. The same verb ten times. Five bullets starting with "Managed" reads as flat as no verbs at all. The grouped lists above exist so each bullet can open differently.
  3. Verbs without substance. "Optimized workflows to drive synergies" opens with a fine verb and says nothing. The verb only works when the "what" is specific and the result is measurable — that's where the keyword match and the credibility both live.
