How ATS Actually Work — No Myths, Just the Mechanics

The resume-tool industry runs on a scary story: robots read your resume, score it, and reject three quarters of applicants before a human ever looks. Parts of that story are real. Parts of it are marketing. Since we sell a resume checker, we owe you the honest version — including what a checker like ours can't do.

An ATS is a database, not a judge

An applicant tracking system — Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, iCIMS, Taleo — is the software a company uses to collect and organize applications. When you apply, three things happen. The system parses your file into structured fields: name, contact info, job titles, dates, skills. It stores that record alongside every other applicant for the role — often hundreds. And then it waits, because the third step belongs to a person: a recruiter opens the pile and searches it, the way you search your email. By keyword, by title, by filter.

That search is the moment that decides most applications. A recruiter with 400 applicants doesn't read 400 resumes; they search "project management," "Salesforce," "RN," and read the resumes that surface. If your resume parsed badly, or describes the same skill in different words than they typed, you're not in the results. Nothing dramatic happened. No robot said no. You're on page nine of a list nobody scrolls to. You're not rejected — you're never found.

The myths, corrected

"75% of resumes are rejected by ATS." You'll see this stat everywhere. It's unsourced — it traces back to a marketing claim from a company that no longer exists, and no ATS vendor publishes anything like it. We used to repeat a version of it too; we've removed it from this site. The honest claim is smaller and worse: there's no reliable number, because most of the loss is invisible. Resumes don't get rejected in bulk — they sit unread in a database because no search ever matched them.

"The ATS auto-rejects weak resumes." Most systems don't auto-reject based on your resume at all. What can disqualify you automatically are knockout questions — the yes/no form fields next to your upload: Are you authorized to work here? Do you hold this license? Can you work this shift? Answer outside the requirement and many systems will screen you out or flag you, regardless of how good your resume is. That's real automation, but it's the application form doing it, not a resume-reading robot.

"Your resume gets an ATS score employers see." No employer sees a score from JobFit, Jobscan, or any other checker. Match scores are a proxy — an estimate of how much of the posting's language your resume contains, which is a stand-in for the real question: would a recruiter's search surface you? A proxy is genuinely useful, the way a practice test is useful. But treat any tool's exact number as gospel — chasing 95 instead of 80 — and you're optimizing a simulation, not your odds.

What a checker can fix — and what it can't

Given the real mechanics, a resume checker can help with exactly two things, and JobFit is built around both. Parsing: tables, columns, graphics, and unusual headings genuinely scramble extraction — a mangled record can't match any search. JobFit flags the formatting patterns that cause it. Wording: recruiters search the posting's vocabulary, so "managed projects" loses to "project management" when that's the phrase they typed. JobFit compares your resume to the posting and shows every term you're missing — the whole diagnosis is free, and it runs entirely in your browser, so your resume is never uploaded anywhere.

Here's what no checker can fix, ours included: being genuinely underqualified for the role. Knockout answers that don't meet a hard requirement. A posting with 800 applicants and an internal favorite. A referral you don't have. Anyone who promises a score guarantees you interviews is selling the myth, not the mechanics. What honest tooling buys you is narrower and still worth having: when you are qualified, you stop losing to a parser error or a wording mismatch.
